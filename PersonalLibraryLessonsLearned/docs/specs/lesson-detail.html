Always validate assumptions before major decisions

üìÖ Added: January 15, 2024 üîÑ Last updated: January 15, 2024 üìä Applied: 3 times High Impact

üìñ What Happened?

During the Q3 planning meeting, I assumed that the entire team was aligned on our strategic priorities based on the lack of objections during the presentation. I moved forward with resource allocation and project assignments based on this assumption.

Two weeks into the quarter, it became apparent that different team members had completely different interpretations of what our priorities meant. The engineering lead thought we were prioritizing infrastructure, while the product manager believed we were focusing on new features. The sales team expected support for customer-facing improvements.

This misalignment led to wasted effort, team frustration, and had to reconvene for an emergency alignment session that should have happened before we started execution.

üí° The Lesson

Never assume alignment exists just because no one objected in a meeting. Silence doesn't mean agreement‚Äîit often means people are processing, confused, or uncomfortable speaking up.

Before starting execution on important initiatives, I need to explicitly verify that everyone understands and agrees with the plan. This means:
  • Asking people to paraphrase the plan back to me in their own words
  • Requesting written confirmation of understanding and agreement
  • Creating space for questions and concerns in a psychologically safe environment
  • Following up individually with key stakeholders, not just in group settings

The cost of explicit validation upfront is minimal compared to the cost of misalignment discovered late in execution.

üéØ How I'm Applying This

My new validation framework includes:

1. Pre-execution validation meeting: Schedule a dedicated session 48 hours after the initial decision to review and confirm understanding. This gap allows people time to process and formulate questions.

2. Written confirmation requirement: All stakeholders must submit a brief written summary of their understanding of the decision, their responsibilities, and any concerns.

3. Individual check-ins: Have one-on-one conversations with key people who seemed quiet or uncertain during group discussions.

4. Assumption documentation: Explicitly list all assumptions we're making and validate each one with the relevant party.

This framework has already prevented two major misalignments in subsequent planning cycles.
